### EV Overcapacity  

by  
Danny Quah  
July 2024  

It is right of course that every nation looks to ensure a level playing field.  All economies, whether it's the EU, the US, China, or everyone else, have legitimate concerns on trade taking place on terms that benefit both sides of each exchange.

The narrative is unclear, however, that is emerging from the US and the EU on the accusations of overcapacity against China.  In this thinking, for strategic sectors such as new energy (electric vehicles EVs; electric batteries; solar panels) Biden has quadrupled US tariffs against China EVs to 100%.  

I understand why there should be intense economic competition in important forward-looking, save-the-planet sectors.  But why also strategic?  Some months ago at an academic conference, I asked why are EVs a matter of national security?  Two Americans jumped on me: "Tanks.  We make the best military attack vehicles when ours are the best automotive technologies."  

In related action the EU proposes NEV (New Energy Vehicle) tariffs of 38% from July.  The reasoning is that this rate, rather than the US's 100%, is what it takes to level the playing field against China's unfair economic practices.  

Unlike concerns surrounding national security---where even magnets and rubber tires are suspect---economic reasoning can be publicly interrogated by drawing on empirical evidence.  

If overcapacity is no more than what the English language says, then it is just capacity arithmetically exceeding use.  For economies, it is excess of supply over demand; it is how much can be produced over what gets consumed.  In international trade, overcapacity in this sense is just exports minus imports, for that is how much more an economy produces than it consumes.  This isn't very useful, however, as then every economy will overcapacity in some sector.  

An alternative view is that overcapacity refers to an unlevel playing field, i.e., where one side doesn't play fair.  That side runs slave labour, practices economic coercion and uses illegally-sourced raw materials, operates low-standard processes, thereby emitting excessive CO2 and pollution; and subsidizes industry or otherwise distorts markets in ways that violate international rules.  It is thus able to produce at cost lower than others who appropriately obey rules.  

Certainly, China is recognised to break WTO regulations.  In June 2024, World Trade Organization (WTO) registered 49 cases against China, with the US raising 23 of those and the EU 11.  However, China is not alone.  Indeed, the WTO recorded 171 cases against the US, with China raising just 18 and the EU, 35.  In other words, the USA has more than three times the cases against it compared to China, whether measured as disputes raised by the rest of the world or by the EU.  

It might well be that China is using dirty energy to produce clean-energy products.  That needs to be documented; I have seen no empirical evidence documenting this.  The further question that should also be asked is, are others using dirty energy this way as well?  Or do others just using dirty energy with no obvious investment for future, cleaner energy usage?  

A more appropriate and more immediate way to look at overcapacity is how economists might naturally do so, i.e., via the concept of dumping: Is the exporter selling in a foreign country at a price lower than it sells in its own, and hence running losses in its own account so as to harm that foreign country?  It is easier to get evidence on this, and that evidence does not support the initial hypothesis.  Many Chinese products---especially batteries and EVs---are cheaper in China than in the EU or the US.  This is hardly surprising in light of the so-called Balassa-Samuelson effect, that says poorer countries are, all else equal, cheaper. 

Are Chinese industries accused of overcapacity making losses?  Market competition in China is fierce.  But many Chinese industries, especially EVs, are profitable in the aggregate.  Some individual firms businesses of course do fail.  But that is just creative destruction, not evidence of dumping.  The Chinese goverment does provide subsidies in the EV industry.  But it does so for consumers to purchase EVs, not for producers to make them.  Those subsidies can purchase Teslas just as easily as BYDs.  Arguably, such consumer subsidies ought to be used more in all other economies to encourage transition to clean-energy vehicles.  

Finally, is China flooding Western markets with clean-energy products?  China's total exports of EVs are only 14% of its aggregate production, a smaller fraction than that of cars exported out of Japan and South Korea production.  And, on the other side of the exchange: What fraction of total new car sales in the US are China-made EVs? Two percent.  China is hardly flooding the world with loss-making EVs.  

Lastly, while rightly looking out for their own industries and businesses, everyone needs to think a little also about the good of our planet.  The world needs clean energy.  To develop, emerging economies still need massive sustained bursts of energy.  They cannot afford costly technologies to replace fossil fuels and, as a result, they will be responsible for most of the world's mpending carbon emissions.  These countries are not agitating about overcapacity: for 70% of humanity, for a long hile still, there is no state of the world where are too many EVs, too much electric battery storage, and too many solar panels.
